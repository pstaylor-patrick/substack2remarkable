The Pulse #155: 5-day RTO looms for those at Big Tech?

Also: senior engineers might be more efficient with AI tools than juniors, Anthropic and Claude Code on a roll, and more.

The Pulse is a series covering events, insights, and trends within Big Tech and startups. Notice an interesting event or trend? Hit reply and share it with me.

Today, we cover:

  1. Could a 5-day RTO be around the corner in Big Tech? From next February, workers at Instagram must be in the office, five days a week. This makes Meta the second tech giant after Amazon to mandate a 5-day RTO. Will more big companies do the same?

  2. Are senior engineers better with AI than juniors are? Data from more than 40,000 users of Cursor suggests that experienced devs get better results from AI agents. It suggests the tool amplifies coding skills not replace them.

  3. Industry Pulse. OpenAI dumps Mixpanel after security breach, Stripe buys usage metering startup, OpenAIs Code Red, a high-risk security issue at a $3B legal startup, and more.

1. Could a 5-day RTO be around the corner for Big Tech?

A year ago, Amazon became the first tech giant to bring staff back into the office for the full five days per week. Back then, I analyzed the reasons for the change, and whether other workplaces would follow suit by dropping the widespread hybrid policy of 2-3 days/week in the office.

Now, Meta employees in the Instagram division have become the latest subjects of a full return to the office, following an announcement by the social media platform this week.

Instagrams 5-day return to office

Instagram employees received the unexpected email on Monday, reports fellow Substacker, Alex Heath, who acquired a copy of the message. It was sent internally by Instagram CEO Adam Mosseri, who wrote:

1. Back to the office: I believe that we are more creative and collaborative when we are together in-person. (...)

2. Fewer meetings: We all spend too much time in meetings that are not effective, and its slowing us down. Every six months, well cancel all recurring meetings and only re-add the ones that are absolutely necessary (...)

3. More demos, less decks: Most product overviews should be prototypes instead of decks.

4. Faster decision-making: Were going to have a more formalized unblocking process with DRIs, and Ill be at the priorities progress unblocking meeting every week.

This decision by Meta affects around a quarter of company staff, and its hard to imagine other divisions not following Instagrams lead; after all, everything in Mosseris memo likely applies across the business.

Five years ago, CEO Mark Zuckerberg predicted 50% of Meta staff would work remotely by now, which didnt happen. Indeed, with Instagrams new 5-day RTO, Id be surprised if 5% of Meta folks work remotely in two years time.

The reason for Instas RTO seems rooted in the leaderships belief that in-office is more productive, as indicated by the top bullet point of Mosseris message. That message in full:

I believe that we are more creative and collaborative when we are together in-person. I felt this pre-COVID and I feel it any time I go to our New York office where the in-person culture is strong.

Starting February 2, Im asking everyone in my rollup based in a US office with assigned desks to come back full time (five days a week). The specifics:

  • Youll still have the flexibility to work from home when you need to, since I recognize there will be times you wont be able to come into the office. I trust you all to use your best judgment in figuring out how to adapt to this schedule.

  • In the NY office, we wont expect you to come back full time until weve alleviated the space constraints. Well share more once we have a better sense of timeline.

  • In MPK [Menlo Park, the HQ], well move from MPK21 to MPK22 on January 26 so everyone has an assigned desk. Were also offering the option to transfer from the MPK to SF office for those people whose commute would be the same or better with that change. Well reach out directly to those people with more info.

  • XFN [cross-functional] partners will continue to follow their own org norms.

  • There is no change for employees who are currently remote.

From what Ive seen of Mosseri from afar, he seems like a pretty straight shooter. Its clear that he feels in-office creates more energy, and in Mosseris defense, I hear similar from many startup founders and leaders who say remote work causes a bunch of headaches: its harder to spot motivational problems and performance issues, information travels more slowly, and rallying teams is harder.

Theres no doubt that running a full-remote company is a lot of effort. Theres often-overlooked labor involved in hiring, onboarding, performance management, team celebrations, and even company-wide meetings none of it is easy.

Linear is a full-remote company with nearly 50 people working there, which recently published details about how it operates. Theyre introducing the concept of coworking hubs, flying in teams for in-person events, and holding regular off-sites, while being careful to hire people who fit the culture.

My feeling is that remote work policies at tech companies are going to become questions of their leaders preferences. Many devs prefer remote work: theres fewer interruptions, more deep focus, and less commuting. Most of us would probably be just as productive and probably more so than when being interrupted in-office.

Leaders who prefer full-remote can cite flexibility and easier hiring from a larger pool of candidates as clear benefits. Meanwhile, those most comfortable with in-person will always have enough reasons to justify a 5-day RTO, along the lines of Mosseris reasoning. Advocates of hybrid setups cite balancing of focus time and efficiency.

In todays job market, any company that pays closer to the top of the market can probably get away with five-days-a-week RTO. Meta is in this space, and although Im sure plenty of devs will dislike the change, the alternative is to go out on the job market, accept a pay cut to join a new company, and start rebuilding your internal network.

Since were in the midst of a weird job market, it makes switching jobs more difficult than before, when the job market was very hot. In this respect, Instagram has external conditions on its side. For devs at Meta, one upside is that Big Tech experience opens more doors, even in this tough job market.

One caveat is that a 5-day RTO is unlikely in places where its hard to hire the right people. So, AI engineers and those working on AI products should be pretty safe, for instance, because those roles are incredibly in-demand, as indicated by the trend of higher base salaries for AI engineers. Based on that, few companies should want to push those workers to quit to join competitors.

2. Are senior engineers better with AI than juniors are?

A recently published paper by the University of Chicago studied 44,160 users of Cursor over the course of 8 months (Oct 2024 - May 2025.) One very interesting finding is that experienced devs accepted code suggestions from their prompts more often than their more junior colleagues did:

This was despite senior engineers using the tool less than junior colleagues:

The more experienced a developer, the more likely theyll accept code from an agent:

The difference is that senior devs prompt agents better, according to the research. The paper analyzes the reasons for the higher acceptance rate:

  • Senior devs are better at breaking up work into smaller tasks that better suit agents

  • They are more skilled at prompting the agent to get what they want

Eric Zakariasson, who works as a software engineer at Cursor, summed up his observations:

Turns out, senior engineers accept more agent output than juniors. This is because:

  • They write higher-signal prompts with tighter spec and minimal ambiguity

  • They decompose work into agent-compatible units

  • They have stronger priors for correctness, making review faster and more accurate

  • Juniors generate plenty but lack the verification heuristics to confidently greenlight output

Shows that coding agents amplify existing engineering skill, not replace it.

Were still early in understanding how to best use AI for coding, and data like this helps show whats happening in the real world.

3. Industry Pulse

OpenAI dumps Mixpanel after poorly-handled security breach

Last week, OpenAI notified customers that its analytics vendor, Mixpanel, had suffered a security breach in which email addresses, names, user IDs, and IP location were exposed. In response to the breach, OpenAI stopped using Mixpanel for any of its services.

Oddly, Mixpanel shared an incredibly short summary of the incident which lacked any meaningful detail, failing to address:

  • How the breach occurred

  • When it started

  • How long detection took, and how the breach was discovered

  • How many customers were impacted

  • What data was lost

Incidents happen, and what matters is how a vendor responds. Do they explain the existing measures they took, and where and why they failed? Are they transparent about the damage, and are they making the correct changes to avoid repeats in the future? Based on OpenAI dumping Mixpanel, it seems that whatever the vendor said was not good enough for the chatbot maker.

The public incident summary by Mixpanel certainly falls short, lacking any specifics and reading more like a press release than an incident document. If I were a customer seeking a secure analytics vendor, the hand-wavey communication would probably cross Mixpanel off my list, too.

Stripe buys usage metering startup

Metronome is a usage-based billing platform that handles metering, pricing, and invoicing for things like API calls, compute, storage, and AI tokens. It has become popular with AI and infra companies that sell based on usage, not on seats.

Now, Stripe is buying the startup for around $1B probably in order to offer more sophisticated, usage-based billing. My guess is that they also get to acquire existing Metronome companies: many of which will be AI businesses like Anthropic, Databricks, or Hugging Face. Interestingly, several of these companies already use Stripe for normal billing, like Anthropic does for monthly payments.

It feels to me that out of all the payment providers, Stripe has been a lot more nimble in building products that AI companies want and need, and the companys clear goal seems to be to become the payments company which AI companies want to use. Congrats to the Metronome team on this exit!

OpenAI declares Code Red

The Information reports:

OpenAI CEO Sam Altman on Monday told employees he was declaring a code red to marshal more resources to improve ChatGPT as threats rise from Google and other artificial intelligence competitors, according to an internal memo.

He didnt specify what was going wrong with ChatGPT, but Google said this fall that its Gemini chatbot had gained ground in terms of usage. Altman recently warned employees privately that Googles AI resurgence could cause temporary economic headwinds for OpenAI.

OpenAIs code red could be a response to ChatGPTs traffic decline while Google Geminis surges, according to Similarweb. Googles Gemini 3 launch Gemini is a very good model that rivals ChatGPT could be causing problems for OpenAI.

My sense is that OpenAI is doing a lot of things at once. In the past six months, the company released Shopping inside ChatGPT, launched a social media app called Sora, launched better image generation with Sora 2, launched new models (GPT-5.1), and was rumored to be working on launching ads.

E-commerce, a new social network, images, and ads, all seem to add up a lot of spinning plates; perhaps a Code Red will help OpenAI prioritize the most important things to work on in the AI race.

Its slightly amusing to recall that Google declared its own Code Red three years ago, in December 2022, one month after ChatGPT was launched. One thing is for sure: theres no lack of competition in AI.

High-risk security issue at $3B legal startup

Filevine is a legal startup valued at $3B, and claims that more than 100,000 legal professionals upload more than 20 million legal documents each day most of which are confidential. The problem is that the platform was insecure, giving admin access with zero authentication to every single document, as discovered and documented by CS student Alex Schapiro.

Alex found the subdomain margolis.filevine.com that was a blank page. After poking around in the source code, Alex located the AWS endpoint dxxxxxx9.execute-api.us-west-2.amazonaws.com/prod. Sending a request to this endpoint returned fully scoped admin tokens to access the Box drive of the legal tech startup exposing confidential customer files. All without authorization!

As soon as Alex realized that the startup had left access to these files open, he disclosed the vulnerability, and Filevine eventually removed the endpoint. But open access to confidential files could have cost the business, big time, if exploited by a bad actor. Alex has advice for businesses:

To companies who feel pressure to rush into the AI craze in their industry be careful!

Always ensure the companies you are giving your most sensitive information to, secure that data.

This was amateurish, negligent engineering by the Filevine team. Then again, how is a legal firm without the technical expertise supposed to assess whether a startup they use is following basic security practices? Incidents like this make it harder for startups to close customers in highly regulated industries.

Chronosphere bought by Palo Alto Networks

Observability startup Chronosphere was bought by Palo Alto Networks for $3.3B. Chronosphere was built on top of Ubers open-sourced observability platform, M3, by one of its creators, software engineer Martin Mao.

We previously covered how Uber built its observability platform with Chronospheres founder, Martin, and also the story of Chronosphere, as told by him. Congrats to the Chronosphere team; its always nice to hear success stories from startups founded by software engineers!

Netflix considers buying Warner Bros.

How things change in a decade:

2012: Time Warner (parent company of Warner Brothers) worth $40B, Netflix worth $5B. Time Warners CEO was asked if Netflix was a threat, and he responded jokingly: Its a little bit like, is the Albanian army [Netflix] going to take over the world? I dont think so.

2025: Netflix worth $440B, Warner Brothers Discovery (the successor of Time Warner) worth $60B, and Netflix is considering buying Warner Brothers.

Looks like the Albanian army is on the march!

Bending Spoons buys Eventbrite (as well)

Speaking of acquisitions, Bending Spoons is on an acquisition roll. Weeks after buying AOL for $1.5B, the company has purchased events company, Eventbrite, for $500M. Bending Spoons already owns Meetup a major competitor to Eventbrite so this purchase gives it more market power in the segment.

Bending Spoons is the most successful tech company out of Italy, even though they operate very differently. They do not build products, but acquire them often letting go of most staff post-acquisition for their in-house devs to take over the running of things. I previously did a podcast with their engineering team and was impressed by how they modernized Evernotes infrastructure, from a strange monolith spread across hundreds of manually provisioned virtual machines, to microservices running on a more cloud-native architecture.

Bending Spoons is also known for raising prices for customers. Its model feels like if an engineering team and private equity had a baby: buy struggling digital businesses that earn decent revenue but cannot turn a profit, then prove they can operate at much lower cost after a full reset.

Whod have thought Bending Spoons would end up owning and operating a roster of famous Silicon Valley brands such as Evernote, Vimeo, Meetup, Eventbrite, and AOL.

4. Anthropic and Claude Code on a roll

While OpenAI has declared a Code Red emergency, Anthropic seems to be doing very well.

Claude Opus 4.5 best coding model yet?

Last week, Anthropic released Claude Opus 4.5, its latest large model. Anthropic calls it the best model in the world for coding which is unsurprising. Independent software engineer Simon Willison found Opus 4.5 to be a great model, but its increasingly hard to tell how much better a new model is, since improvements are incremental, and existing models are already great! As Simon puts it:

My favorite moments in AI are when a new model gives me the ability to do something that simply wasnt possible before. In the past, these have felt a lot more obvious, but today its often very difficult to find concrete examples that differentiate the new generation of models from their predecessors.

Still, Anthropic seems laser-focused on its models being excellent at coding, and their most important customers being developers.

Claude Code passes $1B in ARR

Speaking of important customers, six months after its release, Claude Code has reached $1B in annual run-rate (ARR) revenue. It took ChatGPT 9 months to get to this milestone after its launch, and 2 years for Cursor. With Claude Code, Anthropic may have set the record for fastest-growing product revenue.

There is irony in the most profitable customers of an AI company being software developers who pay for AI; after all, around when ChatGPT launched, several startups positioned themselves as replacements for software developers Devin by Cognition being the highest profile to do so.

Anthropic acquires JavaScript runtime, Bun

Bun is a JavaScript runtime and toolkit that made a big splash at launch, as we covered in The Pulse #62:

Two weeks ago, a JavaScript runtime and toolkit called Bun was released and took the Node.js world by storm. Bun was mostly built by Jared Sumner, a former Stripe engineer, and recipient of the Thiel Fellowship (a grant of $100,000 for young people to drop out of school and build things, founded by venture capitalist, Peter Thiel). Bun has other contributors, but Jared writes the lions share of code.

Bun is a backend JavaScript runtime that is an alternative to Node.js and Deno. Its top focus is performance, and according to benchmarks shared in the launch video, around 10x performance increases can be observed when building packages, running code, or handling inbound requests on a server. This theoretically reduces the hardware requirements of applications.

Bun has stirred up the JavaScript space, and adoption has been rapid. Vendors like Vercel and Replit added support for Bun, as did frameworks like Ruby on Rails and Laravel Sail (PHP.) Many developers are giving Bun a go. Of course, its early days, but Bun keeps improving rapidly.

Bun raised $4M of initial venture funding, and I wondered at the time how it would make money:

Venture funding comes at a cost, and the bill usually falls due later. There is barely anything to complain about in a startup like Oven paying people to work full-time on an open source framework like Bun. Its a win for everyone; the community and the developers working on it full time.

In contrast, the goal of a VC investor is a financial return. VCs fund businesses they expect to eventually be worth a lot more than the value of their investment, and to be either sold, or go public. This means that in the mid to long-term, Buns funding needs to support a business. For most commercial open source companies, the approach tends to be to offer a managed, dedicated service of the open source software, or to provide additional features on top of it.

With Anthropic buying Bun and its team joining Claude Code while still working on Bun theres no longer any question about funding because Anthropic will pay.

Interestingly enough, Bun did not have to join Anthropic. Despite not generating any revenue yet, the company could have started to make money in other ways, according to Buns creator, Jared Sumner, who wrote:

At the time of writing, Buns monthly downloads grew 25% last month (October 2025), passing 7.2 million monthly downloads. We had over 4 years of runway to figure out monetization. We didnt have to join Anthropic.

Instead of putting our users & community through Bun, the VC-backed startups tries to figure out monetization thanks to Anthropic, we can skip that chapter entirely and focus on building the best JavaScript tooling.

Bun will probably become a core part of Claude Code, which is already bundled and shipped as a single-file Bun executable. The team chose Bun for its performance against other JavaScript frameworks. Claude Code also relies on Buns JavaScript and TypeScript runtime, package manager, bundler, and test runner when generating and testing code. So, it makes sense that Anthropic brings this layer in-house.

My sense is that Anthropic remains focused on developers and coding use cases; acquiring Bun helps them make Claude Code better. Anthropics focus is increasingly distinct from OpenAI, where code generation and Codex seem like side projects among many others.

Anthropic to go public before OpenAI?

The Financial Times reports that Anthropic is planning to go public as early as 2026, although opinion is apparently divided.

Anthropic could be prepared to list in 2026, according to one person with knowledge of its plans. Another person close to the company cautioned that an IPO so soon was unlikely.

OpenAI previously floated the possibility of floating on the stock market in 2027. Whats still quite hard to believe is that Anthropic is still only 4 years old: founded in 2021 by none other than seven former OpenAI employees.

Anthropics rise from zero to a $300B valuation in just four years is a good example of how AI has shaken up tech.
